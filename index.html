Audio Reactive Visuals

Exploring the relationship between sound and image

Music visualization is not about making things move when sounds happen. It's about finding the hidden structure in audio and giving it visual form.

This is a collection of experiments exploring how audio features—frequency bands, spectral brightness, rhythmic transients, harmonic content—can drive visual systems in ways that feel alive rather than mechanical.

Each experiment requires audio to run. Load the page, click play, and watch how the visuals respond to the music's structure.

Experiments

Baseline Circle Live
A single circle driven by seven audio features: energy controls size, spectral centroid shifts color temperature, bass adds weight, transients trigger pops, and percussive energy creates jitter.
Frequency Bars Live
Seven frequency bands from sub-bass to brilliance. Bar height follows energy, colors warm-to-cool by frequency, with onset pulses and a spectral centroid indicator.
Particle Physics Live
Audio becomes forces—bass is gravity, mids are wind, highs are turbulence. Particles follow physics; music shapes their world. Onsets trigger bursts.
Chromagram Wheel Live
Harmonic color wheel showing 12 pitch classes. Petals bloom with pitch energy, connections show harmonic relationships, and the wheel rotates on transients.
Flocking Live
Emergent boid behavior with audio-modulated rules. Percussive energy scatters, harmonic content aligns, bass pulls together. Simple rules, complex movement.
Flow Field Live
Perlin noise flow with audio-driven parameters—bass controls curve smoothness, mids drive speed, highs add turbulence. Particles reveal the hidden currents.
Geometric Mandala Live
Sacred geometry with beat-synced rotation. Layered polygons and stars pulse with bass, shimmer with highs, and shift symmetry based on harmonic content.
Reaction Diffusion Live
Gray-Scott simulation with audio-modulated chemistry. Bass feeds growth, mids control pattern complexity, onsets seed new organic formations.

The Approach

All audio analysis happens offline using Python and librosa. We extract features like frequency band energy, spectral centroid, onset strength, chromagram, and harmonic/percussive separation—then export to JSON.

The browser loads this pre-computed data and syncs it to audio playback. This allows heavier analysis than real-time FFT, deterministic playback, and the ability to hand-tune mappings.

Visuals are only as compelling as the mappings that drive them. A sophisticated particle system with naive bass→size mapping feels generic. A simple circle with thoughtful multi-dimensional mapping can feel alive.
